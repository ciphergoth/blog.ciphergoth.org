Most biologists would agree there exist many X which would be likely to crop up if you &mdash; to use Stephen Jay Gould&#8217;s phrase &mdash; re-ran the tape of evolution. Eyes are a very good&nbsp;example.</p> <p>But to get to where Giberson and Miller seem to want to be, you need to have a determinism about evolution which I really don&#8217;t think is tenable. And certainly not where X=humans as we understand&nbsp;them.</p> <p>It seems so unnecessary to me as well &mdash; it&#8217;s very &#8216;God-of-the-gaps&#8217;, trying to squeeze God in to spaces in the existing science. I reckon this is always a Bad Plan because science shifts over time, and it weakens your argument (rhetorically, at least, if not formally) if you have to keep moving your conception of God to fit the gaps in the latest&nbsp;theories. </p> <p>Much better strategically to define your God well outside that frame of reference and have Him properly Transcendent and Omnipotent &mdash; which I think is the sort of thing Liz is doing, and others. Invasive species tend to succeed because they don&#8217;t &#8216;fit in the eco-system they&#8217;re introduced to. Research has shown that they&#8217;re healthier because they leave their parasites and diseases behind (<a rel="nofollow" href="http://www.instadv.ucsb.edu/pa/display.aspx?pkey=761">http://www.instadv.ucsb.edu/pa/display.aspx?pkey=761</a>) and rabbits, in particular, had few predators in Australia in part because the giant birds that might have had them for lunch were already extinct (plausibly because of&nbsp;people).</p> <p>In contrast, in an island ecology, new settlers (it&#8217;s usually birds as they can get there easier) will evolve incredibly rapidly to fill every available niche: Galapagos finches are the canonical example. But filling niches just means learning to eat all the available food (and not get eaten in the process), it doesn&#8217;t have to mean convergent evolution. There&#8217;s more than one way to crack a&nbsp;nut.</p>
